Netflix Top 10 Analysis (Local)

Executive Summary

Leveraging R and Quarto, this project dives deep into Netflix’s Global Top 10 data to decode the strategies behind the platform’s most successful programming. By analyzing massive hits like the Stranger Things franchise, the breakout success of K-Pop: Demon Hunters, and the sustained growth of Korean TV, I explore how release timing, regional demand, and franchise ‘halo effects’ drive global viewership. This analysis moves beyond surface-level rankings to uncover the data-driven trends that define modern streaming success.

Netflix Data

NoteDatasets

For this project, I will be using two datasets from Netflix’s Tudum Website :

  1. The Global Top 10
  2. The Country Top 10

Both data sets are updated on a weekly basis and categorizes programming into four categories:

  • TV (English)
  • TV (Non-English)
  • Flims (English)
  • Films (Non-English)

I. Stranger Things Analysis

Stranger Things Viewership by Season

Code 
# creating Stranger things data frame 
STRANGER_THINGS <- GLOBAL_TOP_10 |>
  filter(show_title == 'Stranger Things') |>
  mutate(season_title = if_else(is.na(season_title), 'Season 1', season_title)) |>
  mutate(season_title = str_replace(season_title, "Stranger Things", "Season")) |>
  select(-category)

# calculating total hours viewed per season.

library(scales)

st_views <- STRANGER_THINGS |>
  group_by(season_title) |>
  summarise(total_hours_view = sum(weekly_hours_viewed),
            runtime = first(runtime))|>
  mutate(total_viewed = total_hours_view/runtime,
         total_hours_viewed = label_number(accuracy = 0.1, scale_cut = cut_short_scale())(total_hours_view),
         total_views = label_number(accuracy = 0.1, scale_cut = cut_short_scale())(total_viewed)) |>
  select(season_title, total_hours_viewed, runtime, total_views) 

st_views |>
  rename(`Runtime(hrs)` = runtime) |>
  format_titles() |>
  datatable(options=list(dom = 't'),
            rownames = FALSE) |>
  formatRound('Runtime(Hrs)', digits = 2)

While the first three seasons accumulated relatively consistent viewership, season 4 garnered almost four times the total hours viewed of season 3.

Because the earlier seasons premiered before 2023, I calculated a Total Views column by by dividing the total hours viewed by runtime to normalize the data. This analysis confirms that even when accounting for Season 4’s longer runtime, the show still attracted a significantly larger audience than previous seasons, proving its growth was driven by increased popularity rather than just longer episodes.

ST_totalweekinchart <- sum(ST_weekinchart$weeks_in_top_10)

With 101 weeks on the Global Top 10, Stranger Things stands as the longest-charting English TV show in Netflix history. This longevity may be partially explained by the recent trend of splitting seasons into multiple parts; Season 4 was the first Stranger Things season to adopt this “multi-part” release and subsequently saw a massive spike in weeks charted. However, Season 5 seems to disprove this release strategy as a guaranteed success factor. Despite being released in three parts, it has actually spent the fewest weeks in the Top 10 of any season so far, suggesting that release timing or audience fatigue may play a larger role than part-splitting alone.

While viewership peaks are expected during new episode releases, this plot reveals a more compelling trend: the significant surge in viewership for previous seasons. With Season 5 arriving three years after its last, the data shows that Season 4 garnered an additional 2.4 Billion hours viewed during the Season 5 release window.

Based on this, I would recommend that programming teams aggressively cross-promote previous seasons—particularly the most recent—at least one month prior to a new premiere. Netflix already leverages this “powerhouse” strategy by headlining franchises on the homepage and utilizing “New Season Coming Soon” banners to prime the audience for a full-series rewatch.
